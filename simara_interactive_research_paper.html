§ 0 — Abstract

Abstract

This study investigates the statistical relationship between fine particulate matter (PM2.5) concentrations, the Air Quality Index (AQI), and respiratory morbidity in Simara, Nepal, over a 359-day observation period spanning the calendar year 2025. Utilising real air quality monitoring data alongside synthetically generated hospital admission records, this research applies Pearson correlation analysis and ordinary least squares linear regression via Python (Pandas, SciPy). The analysis reveals an exceptionally strong correlation between PM2.5 and AQI (r = 0.9376, R² = 0.8791, p < 0.0001), confirming particulate matter as the dominant driver of air quality deterioration at this site. A moderate but statistically significant correlation was identified between AQI and total daily respiratory visits (r = 0.2449, R² = 0.0600, p < 0.0001). Pronounced seasonal variability was observed, with mean winter AQI reaching 97.4 compared to a summer mean of 22.7. Findings are contextualised against 23 peer-reviewed studies sourced via the Consensus academic search engine. The hypothesis that elevated atmospheric particulates are associated with increased respiratory morbidity is supported by both the local dataset and the global literature.

§ 1 — Introduction

Introduction

1.1 Background and Global Context

Air pollution has emerged as one of the most pressing public health challenges of the twenty-first century, particularly in rapidly industrialising South Asia. The WHO estimates that ambient air pollution contributes to approximately 4.2 million premature deaths annually, with lower-middle-income countries bearing a disproportionate burden. Research across 652 cities confirmed that a 10 µg/m³ increase in PM2.5 is associated with a 0.68% rise in daily all-cause mortality and a 0.74% rise in respiratory mortality [7]. In South Asia specifically, an estimated 2 million premature deaths occur annually due to ambient air pollution, with nearly 60% of the population living in areas where PM2.5 concentrations exceed 35 µg/m³ [12].

Nepal, positioned between two of the world's largest industrialising economies, experiences compounding air quality challenges. A study of Nepal's mortality burden found 48,881 (95% CI: 37,175–61,470) adult deaths attributable to PM2.5 in 2019 alone, with the burden consistently increasing from 2000 to 2019 [15]. Simara, in the Terai belt's Bara District, hosts a special economic zone, brick kilns, manufacturing units, and a domestic airport — all significant local emission sources layered upon transboundary pollution from the Indo-Gangetic Plain.

1.2 Defining PM2.5 and AQI

Fine particulate matter (PM2.5 — particles ≤2.5 µm aerodynamic diameter) penetrates pulmonary alveoli and may translocate into the systemic circulation. It encompasses carbonaceous aerosols, sulfates, nitrates, heavy metals, and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons. Studies in the Kathmandu Valley found daily average PM2.5 concentrations ranging from 30 to 207 µg/m³ during the pre-monsoon season, with organic matter (48%) and elemental carbon (13%) as primary constituents [14].

The Air Quality Index (AQI) is a standardised dimensionless scale calculated from concentrations of PM2.5, PM10, O₃, NO₂, SO₂, and CO. At Simara, PM2.5 is the dominant AQI-controlling pollutant, accounting for 87.91% of AQI variance in this dataset.

1.3 Research Hypothesis

H₁: Higher concentrations of atmospheric fine particulate matter (PM2.5) are positively and statistically significantly associated with increased daily respiratory hospital admissions in Simara (α = 0.05).

The null hypothesis H₀ posits no statistically significant linear relationship between PM2.5 and respiratory morbidity outcomes. This study evaluates both using Pearson correlation and OLS regression at significance threshold α = 0.05.

⚠ Data Transparency Statement: AQI and PM2.5 readings are real data from the Simara monitoring station. All health outcome variables (Total Respiratory Visits, Pneumonia, Asthma, COPD, Bronchitis, Other Cases) are synthetically generated for this academic analysis and do not represent actual patient records. All health-related findings should be interpreted as methodological demonstrations.

§ 6 — Discussion

Discussion

6.1 Seasonal Dynamics and Literature Context

The extreme seasonal bimodality of air quality at Simara — winter mean AQI of 97.4 versus summer mean of 22.7 — reflects convergence of reduced boundary layer height, temperature inversions, cessation of precipitation wet deposition, and transboundary biomass burning across the Indo-Gangetic Plain. These dynamics are characteristic of the broader Terai and Gangetic region. Analysis of South Asian air quality trends confirms that Nepal experienced the highest rise in PM2.5 exposure among South Asian nations (87.6 to 99.7 µg/m³ from 1990 to 2017), with unsustainable industrial practices a primary driver [8].

The PM2.5-AQI correlation (r = 0.9376) is consistent with established atmospheric chemistry literature confirming PM2.5 as the dominant criteria pollutant in South Asian semi-urban settings. Research in Kathmandu found organic matter (48%) and elemental carbon (13%) as primary PM2.5 constituents, with combustion sources — biomass burning, vehicle emissions, garbage burning — as the main drivers [14].

6.2 Very Unhealthy Days and Disease-Specific Impacts

The two Very Unhealthy days (AQI 242, 210 — 7–8 December 2025; PM2.5 191.87 and 159.58 µg/m³ respectively) demonstrated disproportionate synthetic health impacts. Mean asthma cases on these days were 127.5 versus a non-extreme baseline of 94.3 (+35.2%), and COPD cases were 65.5 versus 47.9 (+36.7%). These proportional increases align closely with published evidence: a meta-analysis of 12 studies found a 10 µg/m³ PM2.5 increase is associated with a 3.1% rise in COPD hospitalizations [1]. At PM2.5 levels 10–12× the WHO 24h guideline, the cumulative exposure effect would be substantially larger.

For asthma specifically, a systematic review across 22 studies confirmed that PM2.5 is significantly associated with asthma exacerbations (OR: 1.028; 95% CI: 1.009–1.047) [9]. Seasonal analysis confirms winter as the highest-risk window for obstructive airway diseases, with PM2.5 and PM10 levels positively correlated with exacerbations during cold months (r = 0.283 and 0.491, p < 0.05) [2].

6.3 Broader Health and Economic Burden

Research on pneumonia hospitalizations across 184 Chinese cities demonstrated that a 10 µg/m³ increase in 3-day mean PM2.5 is associated with a 0.31% increase in pneumonia admissions, with effects stronger in the elderly [5]. Extrapolating to Simara's context — where winter PM2.5 routinely exceeds 50 µg/m³ — suggests chronic and acute respiratory health risks of significant public health magnitude. The economic burden of transportation-driven air pollution across South Asian cities, including healthcare costs and productivity losses, is described as "considerable" by regional analyses [7C].

6.4 Limitations

Key limitations include: (1) health outcome data are entirely synthetic and do not represent actual clinical observations; (2) the Pearson model assumes linearity, which may not fully capture non-linear dose-response relationships at extreme PM2.5 concentrations; (3) no control for confounders such as temperature, humidity, viral respiratory infections, or day-of-week effects; (4) six missing calendar dates introduce minor temporal gaps (1.6% missingness).
